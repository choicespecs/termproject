A. Task / Problem Definition

1. Introduce your problem

Nicholas McGlynn

Presentations are tried and trusted vehicle in transmitting information. These can be seen commonly in education, business, and social contexts. With the advent of online video conferencing and powerpoint, presentations are shifting towards, boring, unengaging mediums. An article from INC contains the quote "PowerPoint was rated (by online audiences) as no better than verbal presentations with no visual aids. (Ouch.)" BusinessInsider confirms this stipulation. Presentations are no longer the main event of engaging symposiums, but the dreaded part that loses people’s interest and engagement. Issues are presented such as a distinct lack of realtime feedback and assessment of knowledge from the audience, which causes for information to be lost in transit from presenter to user, wasting valuable time and resources to get people to understand. There is a need for an enhancement of the current presentation format: the audience needs to be engaged, and the presenter needs realtime feedback and discovery so that the message being presented is transmitted effectively.

  1. Goal: Instructor desires to know if audience is on task and are knowledgeable of content in focus
  2. Plan: Instructor uses rudimentary functions (i.e. raise hand feature) to try to engage with audiences
  3. Specify: Student will click on raise hand or interrupt instructor to ask question
  4. Perform: Student clicks the unmute or raise hand button
  5. Perceive: Instructor is interrupted and breaks flow of presentation to receive question
  6. Interpret: Instructor, albeit flustered, will know if the audience is on task or is engaging in presentation
  7. Compare: The lack of understanding is resolved, and the presentation may continue

This is a common path of events that has been recently exaggerated due to ongoing COVID-19 pandemic. Instruction is in dire need of an improvement.

A solution is needed, to save time, energy of professionals, as well as sanity!

B. Analysis of Existing Solutions

1. Describe existing solutions.

Christopher Lee

A few ways instructors have tried to combat the issues of feedback and engagement within the hybrid synchronous classroom has been through solutions like online tools, websites, or applications such as Top Hat and Kahoot. Another alternative solution has been utilizing options and tools located within online classrooms like Zoom such as whiteboards, polls, and breakout rooms.

Alternative websites and applications like Top Hat and Kahoot add more engagement in classrooms by providing active learning activities that can be utilized by both online students and in-class students. These activities and websites provide quizzes, polls, and other activities which can engage students in real time, and can also provide a bit of feedback. They help both teachers and students understand the “pace” of the learning within the classroom through carefully selected questions or activities. One problem that arises with these materials is that they typically require students to use their phones to access these applications while they are within the online classroom at the same time. This can be potentially problematic considering that some students may use their phones to connect to their classrooms and could lead to potential technical issues when students are using their phones to both connect to the classroom and access these applications together. Another problem with using these websites and applications is also the complexity or lack of complexity of these applications. The potential uses of both Kahoot and Top Hat require a bit of creativity, experience, and knowledge on behalf of the instructor to use within their classroom. Therefore, it does require a bit of training, practice, and first-hand experience to understand how these tools can be applied within a classroom. This can create a wide spectrum of issues that are not consistent for all instructors. Sometimes these applications can be too complex for instructors to use and sometimes they can be too simple because they lack specific feedback and have limited options in activities for teachers to use for their classrooms.

By having all of the options and tools inside one platform, online classrooms like Zoom can help combat the potential technical problems of students having to access different technologies by having them altogether in one place. However; this does face the same challenges as using alternative websites and applications like Top Hat and Kahoot. These tools and options that are inside online classrooms like Zoom can be somewhat worse compared to alternative websites and applications mainly because they are more limited in the amount of activities they can provide students and the feedback is even more general or is not provided at all to both students and instructors. Therefore, Instructors will need even more training, experience, and creativity to understand how to utilize these options and tools for their classroom.

2. Describe potential guidelines and solutions

Sam Lee

We can use Ben Shneiderman’s paper, Promoting universal usability with multi-layer interface design, as a solution to our issue. Shneiderman’s paper talks about a universal usability/design that would satisfy both first-time users as well as intermittent and expert users. The design is using a multi-layer interface design that enables novice users to begin with a limited set of features at the first layer of the design. They can remain at layer 1, then move up to higher layers when needed or when they have time to learn further features.

This would be a viable solution to our existing solution because we would allow users to use one platform that would layer each option/tool such as Kahoot, Zoom, TopHat, etc. The advantage of layering these features would make the learning curve of having one platform that contains all these features much easier. Just like when you take an exam you wouldn’t want to cram all the information at once, you would want to space out the information so that you would learn over time, and this feature would do just that. (Promoting universal usability with multi-layer interface design)

Keeping Ben Schneiderman’s multi-layer interface design, we could use Guidelines For Designing User Interface Software by Sidney Smith and Jan Mosier. (Guidelines for Designing User Interface Software) They offer guidelines for design of user interface software in six functional areas: data entry, data display, sequence control, user guidance, data transmission, and data protection. The areas we would be studying particularly would be sequence control and user guidance, as implementing Schneiderman’s interface design would focus on them. Sequence control for the movement of each layer and user guidance in making sure that the platform is easy to follow, and progressively gets more complex.

C. Proposed Solutions

1. Propose a Solution.

Tobie Chang

We would like to design an application that affords to not only serve as a platform for communication like lectures between students and teachers, but also gives students and teachers plenty of tools and feedback to increase success on both ends. Our application would build on previous solutions by integrating tools such as whiteboards, quiz and poll capabilities, and breakout rooms all in one app for teachers to take advantage of to address engagement, but would have additional functionalities to remedy our targeted problem of lack of feedback. One of such functionalities would be to, in real-time, provide visual feedback like graphs on quiz results and engagement to both teachers and students, as well as to automatically generate feedback reports for each session and save them for students and teachers to be able to access later in preparation for assessments. The system would aim to achieve user satisfaction on both ends and include appropriate and intuitive signifiers to indicate user capabilities as its interface would closely resemble interfaces our users are already relatively familiar with after engaging with Hybrid Synchronous learning technology during the pandemic.

We recognize that this system seems to endeavor to do a lot. It is essentially an all-inclusive platform for Hybrid Synchronous learning, but we believe having everything in one application is the most convenient solution for our users. In order to assist in universal usability, however, we would also like to propose this system be implemented using Schneiderman’s multi-layered interface design. Users will be able to add and remove capabilities as they desire, making the difficulty adjustable, so that even relatively technologically illiterate users will be able to navigate effectively. We hope that this will give our application a steep (fast) learning curve and further solve difficulties in distance learning.

2. How will you measure success?

Huzaif Sheikh

There are several factors to consider in order to measure the success of the design. To begin, defining success requires a broad understanding of the limitations the design intends to improve on. Comparing functionality with existing solutions is one way to determine design success. Specifically, utilizing features presented in Zoom, TopHat, and Kahoot on the whole will provide classroom clarity and enhanced standards for learning. Certain metrics of value include: activity completion rate, usability scale and feedback scoring, user surveys. These metrics are useful to determine whether or not goals are met and how efficiently the design allows users to complete a task. Due to the complex nature of the design, there are several factors to consider, such as: ease of use, user satisfaction, student retention, instructional support, etc. These factors will be considered throughout the design process and aid in structured integration. Another important measure of success revolves around client / customer interaction. Feedback is a critical tool to enhance the success of the design. Communication with stakeholders enables better implementation and direct results.
